Hypo-skins: A Carbon Ontology for the Housing Crisis

Where human-made mass already exceeds biomass, bizzantine assemblies consume some 40% of total energy production, and the organic legacy emerges as a unique opportunity that needs reorientation from its primary use as fuel. This investigation proposes a carbon ontology that responds to the projected doubling of buildings globally by 2050.

Hypo-skins are portals that bridge the inhabitant, the site, and high-performance carbon materials across space and time. As in the tale of the Mont Blanc ascent, this series of interdisciplinary instances aims to unveil an overlooked contribution, a non-hierarchical, relational, and dynamic diagram that reconnects architecture theory and practice.

Cybermodelling ()


This project builds upon an MIT independent activity period (IAP) workshop titled Topographies of Light, conducted in collaboration with Aileen Iverson () and Andy Payne (McNeel). While the initial attempt aimed to explore differential habitats through deductive reasoning, this subsequent exercise encouraged the set of constraints by which the form could find itself. It consisted of designing a series of hydrophilic elastic shells that not only respond to the possibilities of the material but also to other essential forces, such as gravity, sound, and light. This was pursued through a portal linking a sensed skin (physical interface) with its digital counterpart, resulting in a hybrid "sur le motif" multiplicity of possibilities beyond gestural instinctive approaches. These complex hydrophilic capsules fuse roof and wall, surface and structure, openings and background, unlocking families of antimodernist spaces inhabited beyond projective experiences.

Assamblage: Aileen Iverson, Augusto Gandia, Richa Gupta, Andy Payne

Gradual Assemblies (ETH Zurich, 2020)


This design studio is part of the MAS ETH DFAB and explores , a student from the Media Lab explores m(a)cro scale light artifacts. They start as a series of generic plywood and plexiglass Light Clouds, and evolve into site and time-sensitive Light Sculptures. The design process starts by experiencing different sites while searching for interesting moments of light and history (e.g. the carpenter center of visual arts). These are recorded through computer vision as image schemata of emerging light phenomena, later materialized as inhabitable bays or islands of transparent stacked intensities.
